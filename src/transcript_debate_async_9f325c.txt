# Rigorous Skeptical Synthesis: AI in Criminal Sentencing

I'm going to synthesize the three prior positions, identify where they're vulnerable, and spotlight the deepest unresolved tensions.

## THE CORE TENSION: Three Incompatible Goals

All three positions are trying to solve for three things simultaneously, and they may be **mutually exclusive**:

1. **Consistency** (Engineer's goal): Reduce variation in sentencing for similar cases
2. **Individualization** (Ethicist's goal): Preserve judges' ability to deviate for mercy and context
3. **Bias reduction** (Skeptic's goal): Eliminate discrimination from sentencing

**The problem**: These often *conflict*.

### Why They Conflict

**Scenario**: In a jurisdiction, Black defendants receive 15% longer sentences than white defendants for identical crimes (bias). To reduce consistency variation, you could:

- **Option A**: Standardize upward (bring light sentences up to the median). This maintains historical bias while reducing variation.
- **Option B**: Standardize downward (bring heavy sentences down). This reduces bias but requires judges to *deviate from their historical practice*—which the engineer's tool would flag as "unusual," creating pressure to conform.
- **Option C**: Keep variation high to preserve discretion for mercy. This keeps bias in the system but allows judges to correct it case-by-case.

**None of these is clean.** The three goals are in genuine tension, and the prior positions haven't acknowledged this clearly.

### What's Missing

**Before any tool deployment, you need explicit priority ranking:**
- "Is our primary goal reducing bias, or reducing inconsistency, or preserving discretion?"
- These are *political choices*, not technical ones. They should be made transparently, not buried in tool design.
- Different stakeholders will weight them differently (judges want discretion; victims' advocates may want consistency; defendants want mercy).

---

## SKEPTIC'S VARIANCE DECOMPOSITION: Necessary but Insufficient

The skeptic correctly identified that you need to know *why* sentences vary before you can fix variation. But the proposed audit—"classify variation as legitimate discretion vs. bias"—has a fatal flaw:

### The Classification Problem

**How do you distinguish "legitimate discretion" from "bias" in a sentencing transcript?**

**Example transcript**:
- Judge says: "I'm sentencing you to 60 months because deterrence is important in drug cases, and your prior record shows escalating behavior."
- Fact: This judge sentences Black defendants 18% longer than white defendants for identical priors and offense types.

**Question**: Is this judge exercising legitimate discretion (deterrence philosophy) or exhibiting bias?

**The honest answer**: You can't tell from the transcript alone. The judge's stated reasoning could be:
- Genuinely their sentencing philosophy (legitimate)
- A post-hoc rationalization for a bias they're not aware of (bias)
- Both simultaneously (judges, like all humans, can have biased motivations they sincerely believe are principled)

**Why this matters**: The skeptic's audit proposes classifying cases into "legitimate" vs. "bias" buckets, but the classification itself requires solving the problem you're trying to diagnose. You're trying to use transcripts to identify bias, but the transcripts are the source of the bias.

### What Would Actually Work

Instead of classifying discretion vs. bias from transcripts, you'd need:

1. **Statistical evidence first**: Regression analysis showing demographic disparities *after controlling for case features*. This tells you bias exists in the aggregate.
2. **Only then** examine transcripts: Look at cases where the statistical model predicts a sentence but the judge deviated significantly. *Those* are the cases where you can investigate whether the deviation was legitimate or biased.
3. **Never assume** a judge's stated reasoning proves legitimacy. A judge who says "I value rehabilitation" but consistently sentences minority defendants longer is not exercising legitimate discretion—they're rationalizing bias.

**Implication**: The skeptic's mitigation is a step in the right direction, but it needs to be inverted: start with statistical bias detection, then use transcripts to understand *why* bias exists, not to classify it away.

# Ethical Analysis: AI in Criminal Sentencing

## 1. THE ASYMMETRY OF HARM (Who Bears the Risk?)

### The Core Problem

Any sentencing tool creates a **structural imbalance in who benefits and who bears consequences**:

- **Judges** gain: clearer reasoning, potential legal cover, reduced cognitive load
- **Defendants** lose: individualized mercy, the ability to argue "my case is different," protection against anchoring bias
- **Crime victims** are entirely absent from design conversations (their interests assumed, never directly consulted)
- **Communities** bear the long-term cost if the tool locks in historical injustices

**Concrete scenario**: A 24-year-old with no priors, severe mental illness, and an abusive childhood would have received 28 months from a judge who values rehabilitation. Post-tool, that judge sees "similar offense cases: 42-66 months, median 54." The judge isn't *forced* to sentence to 54, but the tool creates **psychological anchoring**—deviating downward now feels like an exception requiring justification, rather than a legitimate exercise of discretion. The defendant loses 26 months of their young life because they were sentenced *after* the tool existed.

**Why this matters ethically**: Defendants have a constitutional right to individualized sentencing. A tool that makes mercy feel exceptional rather than normal undermines that right—even if it doesn't formally eliminate it. And crucially: **defendants don't choose whether the tool is used. The judge does.**

### Ethical Requirement 1: Reverse the Design Burden

The tool should be built to **expand** judicial options, not narrow them:

**Instead of showing consistency anchors, show:**
- "Here are 8 cases with similar facts where judges sentenced *below* the median (28-40 months). Here's what they said about why mercy was appropriate."
- "This defendant has no prior record, unlike 73% of comparable cases. Judges cite that as a reason to deviate downward. Is it relevant here?"
- "You've personally deviated below the median in 12% of similar cases over your tenure. Here's the reasoning you gave. Does it apply here?"

**The principle**: Make it *easy* to see why a case warrants deviation, not why it warrants conformity.

### Ethical Requirement 2: Genuine Defendant Voice in Design

Before deployment, conduct structured stakeholder input with **actual decision-making power**:

- **Public defenders**: "Does this tool help or harm your ability to argue for individualized sentences? Would your clients prefer full judicial discretion or a judge using this tool?"
- **Sentencing reform organizations**: "Does this create space to challenge injustices or lock them in?"
- **Defendants themselves** (through surveys or focus groups): "Would you trust a judge using this system?"

**Critical**: If any of these groups says "this constrains our ability to argue for mercy," that's not just a concern to document—it's evidence the tool *increases* risk for defendants. The burden then shifts to proponents to fundamentally redesign it.

### Ethical Requirement 3: Explicit Appeal Rights for Tool-Influenced Sentences

If a tool shaped sentencing, defendants get a dedicated right to challenge the process:

- "Your Honor, the tool compared my case to case #4521, but I differ in X ways. Can we examine whether that comparison was apt?"
- This is narrower than a full resentencing appeal—it's a right to contest the *process* that informed the judge's reasoning.
- Appellate courts should develop precedent: "Sentences influenced by consistency tools are subject to heightened scrutiny on whether case comparisons were fair."

**Why this matters**: It creates accountability. If judges know sentences influenced by tools will face closer appellate review, they'll be more cautious about letting the tool anchor them.
