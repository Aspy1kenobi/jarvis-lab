## **The Core Ethical Problem: Dignity Under Opacity**

The deepest issue isn't bias or explainability—it's that **a person's freedom should not be constrained by a system they cannot meaningfully contest.**

This is distinct from other high-stakes AI decisions:
- A loan denial is harmful but reversible; you can reapply or go elsewhere
- A hiring rejection is unfair but you can interview elsewhere
- A criminal sentence is **irreversible in its core harm**—years of your life, separated from family, with permanent collateral consequences

**The ethical principle at stake**: Due process requires that a person can understand and challenge the reasoning behind their punishment. Not perfectly—judges have discretion. But *intelligibly*.

An AI system that influences sentencing violates this if:
1. The defendant cannot understand why it recommended their sentence
2. The defendant cannot effectively argue against it (because they don't know what to argue against)
3. The judge treats it as authoritative rather than advisory (which the skeptic rightly flags will happen)

**This is an autonomy problem, not just a fairness problem.**

### The asymmetry problem:
**Benefits flow to institutions; harms flow to individuals.** The defendant has the most at stake and the least power in this system.

- Prior criminal record → longer sentence

- Overpolicing in certain communities → more prior records
- Redlining → concentration of poverty → higher crime rates
- Structural unemployment → employment disparities

**The ethical problem**: The system launders systemic racism through "neutral" features. It's not the model's fault—it's what the data contains. But deploying the model *automates and legitimizes* that laundering.

**Fairness principle violated**: Equal treatment. A defendant in a low-income, heavily-policed neighborhood gets a harsher recommendation not because of their crime, but because of where they live—a factor beyond their control and rooted in historical injustice.

- Recommendations shape sentences
- Sentences become training data for the next version of the model

- The cycle tightens

**This is not a technical problem.** It's a justice problem. The system becomes self-reinforcing, and each iteration makes it harder to escape.

One argument for AI is "consistency"—similar cases should be treated similarly. This sounds fair.

**But it's only fair if the baseline is just.** If the baseline sentencing data reflects centuries of racism, discrimination, and unequal treatment, then "consistency" means *consistently unjust*.

Making injustice consistent doesn't make it fair; it makes it systematic.

### The Distinction That Actually Matters

**Judicial discretion** (current system):
- A judge sentences you based on factors she considers relevant
- She writes a reasoned opinion explaining her decision
- You can appeal on grounds that the sentence is "unreasonable" or that the judge misapplied law
- The reasoning is *contestable in principle*—you can argue "the judge wrongly weighted my employment status" or "she ignored mitigating evidence"

**AI recommendation** (proposed system):
- A system scores you on 47 variables, some opaque, some proprietary
- The judge reads a number: "recommended 8-12 years"
- The judge writes: "I considered the AI recommendation and imposed 9 years"
- You cannot appeal on grounds that "the AI wrongly weighted my zip code" because:
  - You don't know what variables it used
  - You don't know the weights
  - The vendor claims trade secret protection
  - Even if you knew, the AI's reasoning isn't "law"—it's pattern-matching

**The legal difference**: A judge's reasoning can be *wrong in principle* (she misapplied the law). An AI's recommendation can only be *wrong in fact* (it mispredicted). You cannot contest a prediction; you can only contest an interpretation of law.

**This is not solvable by transparency alone.** Even if we know the AI's inputs and weights, we still cannot meaningfully contest it because contestability requires a framework of *legal principle*, not just statistical accuracy.

**The ethical inversion**: The system that was supposed to constrain AI becomes a mechanism to enforce it. The judge becomes the outlier who must justify herself against the "objective" system.

# Rigorous Skeptical Pressure Test: AI in Criminal Sentencing

I'm going to systematically examine the assumptions across all three prior positions—including where the skeptical critiques themselves may be overconfident.

## **CRITICAL ASSUMPTION #1: "Contestability" Is the Right Frame, But It May Be Unfalsifiable**

### The Problem:

The Ethicist has correctly identified that contestability is the crux. But the definition is now so precise that it may have become *circular*—unfalsifiable in a way that predetermines the conclusion.

**The specific risk:**

The Ethicist defines contestability as requiring "a framework of legal principle (not just statistical accuracy)." This means:
- A judge's reasoning can be contested because it invokes law
- An AI's reasoning cannot be contested because it invokes patterns, not law

**But this assumes a sharp distinction that may not exist:**

What if we redesigned the AI system to *output legal reasoning*? For example:
- AI identifies that a defendant has no prior record (fact)
- AI notes that the sentencing guideline for this crime is 5-10 years (law)
- AI recommends 7 years based on the guideline and the defendant's record (reasoning)

Is this now "contestable in principle"? The defendant can argue: "The guideline doesn't apply to me because [legal reason]." The AI's recommendation is now grounded in law, not just pattern-matching.

**If the answer is "yes, that's contestable,"** then the problem isn't AI—it's *opaque* AI. And the solution is transparency + legal reasoning, not exclusion.

**If the answer is "no, it's still not contestable because an AI can't interpret law,"** then we've smuggled in an assumption: *Only humans can legitimately exercise legal judgment.* This is a philosophical claim about the nature of legal reasoning, not a technical one. It needs defending on its own terms.

**What would need to be true:**
- A clear definition of what makes reasoning "legal" vs. "statistical" that doesn't collapse under scrutiny
- Evidence that a defendant can actually contest a judge's legal reasoning in current appellate practice (this is weaker than the Ethicist assumes—appellate courts defer heavily to trial judges)

---

## **CRITICAL ASSUMPTION #2: "Constrained Use Cannot Be Enforced" Assumes Enforcement Is Transparent**

### The Problem:

The Skeptic (and Planner) correctly identify that judicial deference is invisible—you can't tell if a judge is rubber-stamping or exercising discretion. But this assumes the *only* way to enforce constraints is through direct observation.

**What's missing:**

There are indirect enforcement mechanisms that work in other domains:

1. **Appellate review with teeth.** If appellate courts actively review sentences for *unexplained conformity* to AI recommendations (i.e., "the judge sentenced exactly as the AI recommended without articulated reasons"), that creates deterrence. Judges know they'll be reversed if they appear to defer. This requires appellate courts to change their practice, but it's not impossible.

2. **Sentencing commissions can audit compliance.** Many states have sentencing commissions that collect data on all sentencing decisions. They can identify patterns: "Judges in District X sentence 95% within the AI recommendation band, while judges in District Y sentence 60% within the band." This doesn't tell you *why*, but it flags anomalies for investigation.

3. **Defense bar mobilization.** If every defense attorney routinely challenges sentences by arguing "the judge improperly deferred to the AI recommendation," that creates a cost for judges. Even if the challenges fail, they consume judicial time and create a record.

**The risk I'm identifying:**

The Skeptic conflates "you can't directly observe judicial deference" with "it cannot be constrained." These are different. Enforcement may be *harder* and *less reliable* than for other rules, but not impossible.

**What would need to be true:**
- Evidence from bail/parole that "constrained AI use" has actually degraded over time (if it has, that supports the Skeptic; if not, that challenges them)
- A realistic model of appellate review that could detect systematic deference without requiring mind-reading
- Data on whether appellate courts would actually reverse for "improper reliance on AI"—this is an empirical question, not settled

# Ethical Analysis: AI in Criminal Sentencing

I'm reading the skeptic's and planner's contributions carefully. They've identified real technical and political failure modes. ## **The Core Ethical Problem: Dignity Under Irreversible Harm**

The deepest issue isn't technical bias or explainability—it's that **a person's freedom should not be constrained by a system they cannot meaningfully contest, especially when the harm is irreversible and the person has no power in the system.**

This is categorically different from other high-stakes AI decisions:
- A loan denial is harmful but *reversible*; you can reapply next year
- A hiring rejection is unfair but *fungible*; you can apply elsewhere
- A criminal sentence is **irreversible in its core harm**—years of your life, separated from family, with permanent collateral consequences (employment, housing, voting rights, family separation)

**The ethical principle at stake**: Due process requires that a person can understand and *intelligibly contest* the reasoning behind their punishment. Not perfectly—judges have discretion. But in a way that engages with law and principle, not just statistical patterns.

An AI system that influences sentencing violates this because:

1. **The defendant cannot understand why it recommended their sentence.** Even with full transparency about inputs and weights, a neural network's reasoning is not intelligible in the way legal reasoning is. A defendant can argue "the judge wrongly weighted my employment status"—that's a legal argument. They cannot argue "the AI wrongly weighted the interaction between zip code and prior arrests"—that's not a legal principle; it's a statistical artifact.

2. **The defendant cannot effectively argue against it.** They don't know what to contest. The system is a black box (even if technically explainable). The vendor claims trade secret protection. Even if they knew everything, they have no legal framework for saying "this reasoning is unjust."

3. **The judge will treat it as authoritative even if it's officially advisory.** The skeptic is right that this is cognitive bias at scale, not misconduct. A judge reads "recommended 8-12 years" before sentencing. That anchor shapes her decision-making in her own mind, even as she believes she's exercising independent judgment. This is invisible and unauditable.

**This is an autonomy problem, not just a technical fairness problem.** The defendant is treated as an object to be scored, not a person to be heard.

## **Stakeholder Analysis: Who Benefits, Who Bears Risk?**

### Who benefits:
- **Court administrators**: Faster dockets, reduced judicial burden, less discretionary variation
- **Prosecutors**: Predictability in plea negotiations; better ability to model case outcomes
- **Some judges**: Cover for difficult decisions ("the evidence-based system recommended this"); reduced accountability for harsh sentences
- **Victims' advocates** (potentially): Consistency in how serious crimes are treated
- **Tech vendors**: Market expansion, recurring contracts

### Who bears the risk:
- **Defendants** (overwhelmingly): Loss of meaningful contestability; risk of harsher sentences if AI amplifies historical disparities; permanent collateral consequences
- **Public defenders**: Already overworked; now must also audit AI recommendations and argue against opaque systems with fewer resources than prosecutors
- **Specific communities**: Disparate impact if system trained on biased historical data (heavily-policed neighborhoods, racial minorities, poor communities)
- **Judicial independence**: Judges become administrators of AI rather than exercisers of reasoned judgment
- **The legitimacy of the criminal justice system itself**: If sentencing is perceived as algorithmic rather than human judgment, trust erodes—especially for communities already skeptical of the system

### **The asymmetry problem** (the crux):
**Benefits flow to institutions; harms flow to individuals.** The defendant has the most at stake and the least power in this system. They cannot opt out, cannot appeal to another jurisdiction, cannot hire better experts to audit the AI. The burden of contestability falls entirely on them.

This inverts the ethical burden of proof: **The case for AI must be overwhelming to justify removing a defendant's ability to contest their sentence. It is not overwhelming. Therefore, it should not be deployed.**
